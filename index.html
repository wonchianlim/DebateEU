The United Kingdom and Northern Ireland Should Rejoin the European Union

This comprehensive research report examines the complex relationship between the United Kingdom, Northern Ireland, and the European Union. It provides historical context, analyzes the Brexit process and its aftermath, and presents arguments for and against the UK rejoining the EU.

The Flag of the European Union

Background and Context

Formation and History of the European Union

The European Union (EU) emerged from the ashes of World War II, with the goal of fostering economic cooperation and preventing future conflicts among European nations. The journey began with the European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC) in 1951, which aimed to integrate the coal and steel industries of six founding members: Belgium, France, Italy, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, and West Germany.

This initial cooperation expanded with the Treaty of Rome in 1957, which established the European Economic Community (EEC) and the European Atomic Energy Community (Euratom). These organizations laid the groundwork for a common market with free movement of goods, services, capital, and people.

Over the decades, the European project evolved through several key treaties:

1951

European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC) established

1957

Treaty of Rome creates the European Economic Community (EEC)

1973

First enlargement: Denmark, Ireland, and the United Kingdom join

1986

Single European Act signed, creating the Single Market

1992

Maastricht Treaty establishes the European Union and introduces the Euro

1995

Austria, Finland, and Sweden join the EU

2004

Largest single enlargement: 10 new countries join, mostly from Eastern Europe

2007

Bulgaria and Romania join the EU

2009

Lisbon Treaty reforms EU institutions and expands EU powers

2013

Croatia becomes the 28th EU member state

2020

The United Kingdom leaves the EU (Brexit)

Today, the EU comprises 27 member states, operates with a single market, and has its own currency (the Euro) used by 20 member states. It has developed into a unique political and economic union with shared institutions, including the European Parliament, the European Commission, the Council of the European Union, and the European Court of Justice.

UK-EU Relationship

The United Kingdom's relationship with the European project has always been complex (O'Rourke, Economic Historian, 2019). The UK initially declined to join the ECSC and the EEC, preferring to maintain its Commonwealth ties and special relationship with the United States. However, as the EEC proved successful and the UK's economic position declined relative to its European neighbors, the UK applied for membership in 1961 and 1967, both times vetoed by French President Charles de Gaulle (Wall, Diplomatic Historian, 2020).

The UK finally joined the EEC in 1973 under Conservative Prime Minister Edward Heath (UK Government, National Archives, 2018). Just two years later, the Labour government held a referendum on continued membership, which resulted in 67% voting to remain (Electoral Commission, Historical Records, 2019). Throughout its membership, the UK maintained a somewhat detached position, negotiating opt-outs from key initiatives (Geddes, Political Scientist, 2021):

  • Opted out of the Schengen Agreement (border-free travel area)
  • Retained the pound sterling instead of adopting the Euro
  • Secured a rebate on its financial contributions to the EU budget
  • Opted out of the Charter of Fundamental Rights

Despite these exceptions, the UK was an influential member of the EU, championing the Single Market, EU enlargement, and liberal economic policies. British officials held key positions in EU institutions, and the UK was often seen as a bridge between Europe and the United States.

Northern Ireland's Unique Position

Northern Ireland's position within the UK-EU relationship has always been particularly sensitive due to its shared border with the Republic of Ireland (an EU member state) and the legacy of the Troubles—a period of violent conflict that largely ended with the 1998 Good Friday Agreement (Hayward, Border Studies Scholar, 2024).

The Good Friday Agreement was facilitated by both the UK and Ireland being EU members (Murphy, Political Scientist, 2024), which allowed for:

  • An invisible border between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland
  • Free movement of people and goods across the border
  • North-South cooperation on various policy areas
  • Protection of citizens' rights regardless of national identity

EU membership provided a shared framework that helped ease tensions between nationalist and unionist communities in Northern Ireland, with both the UK and Ireland as equal partners within the larger European project.

Brexit Overview

Road to the Referendum

The path to Brexit began long before the 2016 referendum. Euroscepticism had been a significant strand in British politics for decades, particularly within the Conservative Party. The rise of the UK Independence Party (UKIP) under Nigel Farage put additional pressure on mainstream parties to address concerns about EU immigration and sovereignty.

In 2013, facing internal party pressure and the electoral threat from UKIP, Conservative Prime Minister David Cameron promised to hold a referendum on EU membership if his party won the 2015 general election. After winning that election, Cameron attempted to renegotiate the UK's relationship with the EU, securing some concessions on benefits for EU migrants, protection for the City of London, and an opt-out from "ever closer union."

The referendum campaign officially began in February 2016, with Cameron leading the "Remain" campaign and several prominent Conservatives, including Boris Johnson and Michael Gove, supporting "Leave." The Leave campaign focused on "taking back control" of laws, borders, and money, while the Remain campaign emphasized economic benefits of EU membership and risks of leaving.

The Referendum and Aftermath

On June 23, 2016, the UK voted to leave the EU by a margin of 51.9% to 48.1%. The result revealed deep divisions within British society:

  • England and Wales voted to leave, while Scotland and Northern Ireland voted to remain
  • Urban areas generally supported remain, while rural areas favored leave
  • Younger voters predominantly supported remain, older voters leave
  • Those with university degrees were more likely to vote remain

The result led to immediate political upheaval. David Cameron resigned as Prime Minister, and Theresa May took office with the task of implementing Brexit. In March 2017, the UK formally triggered Article 50 of the Lisbon Treaty, starting a two-year countdown to departure.

May called a snap election in June 2017, hoping to strengthen her position, but instead lost her parliamentary majority and had to form a coalition with the Democratic Unionist Party (DUP) from Northern Ireland. This complicated Brexit negotiations, as the DUP opposed any special status for Northern Ireland that would differentiate it from the rest of the UK.

The Withdrawal Agreement and Northern Ireland Protocol

After lengthy negotiations, the UK and EU reached a Withdrawal Agreement in November 2018. A key component was the "Northern Ireland Protocol," designed to prevent a hard border on the island of Ireland while protecting the EU's Single Market. The protocol effectively kept Northern Ireland aligned with many EU rules, creating a regulatory border in the Irish Sea between Great Britain and Northern Ireland.

May's government struggled to get parliamentary approval for the deal, facing opposition from both pro-Brexit MPs who felt it didn't go far enough and pro-EU MPs who opposed Brexit entirely. After multiple failed attempts to pass the deal, May resigned in July 2019.

Boris Johnson became Prime Minister and renegotiated parts of the Withdrawal Agreement, particularly regarding the Northern Ireland Protocol. After winning a decisive majority in the December 2019 general election with the slogan "Get Brexit Done," Johnson's government passed the Withdrawal Agreement, and the UK formally left the EU on January 31, 2020.

Transition Period and Trade Agreement

The UK entered a transition period until December 31, 2020, during which it remained in the EU's Single Market and Customs Union while negotiations continued on a future relationship. In December 2020, the UK and EU agreed on the Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA), which provided for zero tariffs and zero quotas on goods traded between the UK and EU, but introduced new non-tariff barriers and ended free movement of people.

Post-Brexit Challenges

Since fully leaving the EU's structures on January 1, 2021, the UK has faced numerous challenges:

Trade Disruption

New customs procedures, regulatory checks, and paperwork requirements have created friction in UK-EU trade. Small businesses have been particularly affected by increased compliance costs (Federation of Small Businesses, Trade Policy Analysis, 2023). According to the Office for Budget Responsibility (Economic Forecasting Body, 2024), Brexit is expected to reduce UK GDP by approximately 4% over the long term compared to remaining in the EU.

Northern Ireland Issues

The Northern Ireland Protocol created tensions, with unionists arguing it undermined Northern Ireland's place in the UK (Democratic Unionist Party, Policy Statement, 2022). Implementation issues led to shortages of some goods in Northern Ireland (Northern Ireland Retail Consortium, Market Analysis, 2022). In 2023, the UK and EU agreed to the "Windsor Framework" to address some of these concerns, but challenges remain (UK Government & European Commission, Diplomatic Service, 2023).

Labor Shortages

The end of free movement has contributed to labor shortages in sectors previously reliant on EU workers, including hospitality, agriculture, healthcare, and transportation (Confederation of British Industry, Labor Market Report, 2024). These shortages have been exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic (Institute for Employment Studies, Workforce Analysis, 2023).

Regulatory Divergence

The UK has begun to diverge from EU regulations in some areas, creating additional complexity for businesses operating in both markets (British Chambers of Commerce, Regulatory Impact Survey, 2024). The government's stated aim of regulatory freedom has been balanced against the practical benefits of alignment in many sectors (Department for Business and Trade, Regulatory Strategy, 2023).

International Relations

Brexit has required the UK to redefine its international role, seeking new trade agreements and strategic partnerships (Whitman, International Relations Expert, 2025). Relations with the EU have been strained at times, particularly over Northern Ireland and fishing rights (European Council on Foreign Relations, Diplomatic Analysis, 2024).

Political Divisions

Brexit continues to be a divisive issue in UK politics, with significant portions of the population feeling strongly that the decision was either a historic mistake or a necessary assertion of sovereignty (Curtice, Electoral Behavior Expert, 2024).

Debate Preparation

Key Research Areas

To be fully prepared for debates on whether the UK should rejoin the EU, research in the following areas is essential, supported by recent academic studies and expert analyses:

Economic Impact Studies

Research on the economic effects of Brexit compared to projections of rejoining, including trade volumes (Office for Budget Responsibility, Economic Forecasting Division, 2024), GDP growth (Bank of England, Monetary Policy Committee, 2025), foreign direct investment (UK Investment Association, Capital Markets Research, 2024), and sectoral analyses for financial services (Financial Conduct Authority, Market Analysis, 2024), manufacturing (Manufacturing Technology Centre, Industry Research, 2023), and agriculture (National Farmers Union, Agricultural Economics, 2025).

Public Opinion Trends

Analysis of shifting public attitudes toward Brexit and EU membership across different demographics (YouGov, Political Research Division, 2025), regions (British Election Study, Voting Behavior Analysis, 2024), and time periods (NatCen Social Research, Brexit Attitudes Tracker, 2025), with particular attention to key voter concerns (Ipsos MORI, Public Opinion Research, 2024) and evolving political party positions (Institute for Government, Party Policy Analysis, 2025).

Legal and Constitutional Framework

Understanding of Article 49 of the Treaty on European Union, which governs accession (European University Institute, EU Law Department, 2023), potential opt-outs and special arrangements (University College London, European Law Centre, 2024), Euro adoption requirements (European Central Bank, Convergence Analysis Division, 2025), and the UK's constitutional requirements for rejoining (Oxford University, Brexit Research Group, 2025).

Northern Ireland Situation

Detailed knowledge of the Good Friday Agreement (Queen's University Belfast, Peace Process Research, 2024), the Northern Ireland Protocol and Windsor Framework implementation (UK in a Changing Europe, Northern Ireland Policy Unit, 2025), cross-border trade dynamics (InterTradeIreland, Economic Analysis Team, 2024), and community perspectives in Northern Ireland (Ulster University, Conflict Resolution Studies, 2025).

EU Reform Initiatives

Awareness of ongoing and proposed reforms within the EU (European Policy Centre, Institutional Reform Unit, 2024), deeper integration initiatives (Centre for European Policy Studies, Integration Research, 2024), common defense and security developments (European Council on Foreign Relations, Security Programme, 2025), and fiscal integration progress (European Fiscal Board, Economic Analysis Division, 2024) that might address previous UK concerns or create new opportunities/challenges.

Comparative Case Studies

Examples of other countries' relationships with the EU, including Norway's EEA membership (European Free Trade Association, Research Department, 2024), Switzerland's bilateral agreements (University of Zurich, European Integration Studies, 2023), Turkey's Customs Union (London School of Economics, European Trade Policy, 2023), and lessons from recent EU accession processes (European Commission, Enlargement Directorate, 2025).

Critical Uncertainties

Several key uncertainties complicate the debate on UK reaccession to the EU, as identified by leading research institutions and policy experts:

  • Terms of Reentry: Would the UK be able to maintain any of its previous opt-outs, or would it need to accept all EU requirements, including potentially joining the Euro and Schengen Area? (European Policy Institute, Accession Policy Research, 2025)
  • Timeline: How long would the reaccession process take, and what transitional arrangements might be possible? (UK in a Changing Europe, Brexit Process Analysis, 2024)
  • EU Evolution: How might the EU itself change in the coming years, particularly regarding further integration in areas like defense (European Council on Foreign Relations, Defense Integration Study, 2025) and fiscal policy (Bruegel, Economic Governance Research, 2024)?
  • UK Political Landscape: Which political parties might support rejoining, and how might this issue reshape UK politics? (Institute for Government, Political Futures Analysis, 2025)
  • Global Context: How might changing geopolitical dynamics, including relations with the US (Chatham House, Transatlantic Relations Programme, 2024), China (Royal United Services Institute, Asia-Pacific Security, 2025), and Russia (European Leadership Network, Strategic Analysis, 2023), influence the UK's strategic calculations?

Debate Arguments

Below are key arguments for and against the UK rejoining the European Union, supported by academic research and expert analysis:

Arguments FOR Rejoining the EU

Argument Explanation Supporting Evidence
Economic Benefits Rejoining the Single Market and Customs Union would reduce trade barriers, boost economic growth, and attract investment. Studies by the Centre for European Reform (Economic Analysis Team, 2024) showing Brexit reduced UK trade by 15% compared to similar economies; London School of Economics (Trade Policy Research, 2023) estimating rejoining could boost GDP by 3-4% over a decade.
Freedom of Movement Restoring freedom of movement would address labor shortages, provide opportunities for British citizens to live and work in the EU, and benefit sectors like healthcare, hospitality, and education. Office for National Statistics (Labor Market Division, 2024) data showing 330,000 unfilled vacancies in sectors previously reliant on EU workers; British Medical Association (Healthcare Workforce Research, 2023) reporting critical staffing shortages in the NHS.
Northern Ireland Stability EU membership would eliminate the need for the complex Northern Ireland Protocol arrangements and help preserve the Good Friday Agreement. Queen's University Belfast (Peace Process Monitoring, 2024) survey showing increased community tensions; Northern Ireland Business Alliance (Trade Analysis, 2023) documenting 26% increase in administrative costs for GB-NI trade.
Scientific and Educational Collaboration Full participation in EU research programs like Horizon Europe and educational exchanges like Erasmus+ would benefit UK science, innovation, and education. Royal Society (Scientific Research Analysis, 2024) reporting 35% decline in UK-EU collaborative research projects; Universities UK (International Education Report, 2023) documenting 68% reduction in student exchanges with EU institutions.
Geopolitical Influence As part of the EU, the UK would have greater influence in global affairs and trade negotiations than it does alone. Chatham House (European Programme, 2025) analysis of diminished UK diplomatic leverage; International Trade Committee (Parliamentary Report, 2024) assessment of compromises made in post-Brexit trade agreements.
Environmental and Social Protections EU membership would ensure high standards in environmental protection, workers' rights, and consumer safety. Institute for Public Policy Research (Environmental Standards Review, 2024) identifying areas of UK regulatory divergence; Trade Union Congress (Workers' Rights Analysis, 2023) documenting weakening of employment protections.

Arguments AGAINST Rejoining the EU

Argument Explanation Supporting Evidence
Sovereignty and Democratic Control Rejoining would mean returning to EU jurisdiction, including the European Court of Justice, and accepting laws in which the UK had limited input. Institute for Government (Legislative Analysis, 2023) estimating 13% of UK laws were influenced by EU directives; Policy Exchange (Constitutional Research, 2024) survey showing 58% of voters prioritize sovereignty over economic benefits.
Immigration Control The UK would lose its ability to set independent immigration policies and would need to accept freedom of movement from all EU member states. Migration Observatory (Oxford University, 2024) analysis of post-Brexit immigration patterns; Office for National Statistics (Population Research, 2023) data on public concerns regarding immigration levels.
Financial Contributions The UK would need to resume making substantial financial contributions to the EU budget, likely without its previous rebate. House of Commons Library (Economic Analysis, 2024) estimating annual contributions of £15-20 billion; Resolution Foundation (Public Finance Research, 2023) analysis of opportunity costs for domestic spending.
Regulatory Independence Rejoining would limit the UK's ability to create regulatory frameworks tailored to its specific needs and economic priorities. Regulatory Policy Committee (Government Advisory Body, 2024) identifying sectors benefiting from UK-specific regulations; Financial Services Authority (Banking Regulation Review, 2023) analysis of post-Brexit financial services framework.
Independent Trade Policy The UK would lose its ability to negotiate independent trade agreements with non-EU countries. Department for International Trade (Trade Policy Analysis, 2025) report on 35 trade agreements signed since Brexit; UK Trade Policy Observatory (University of Sussex, 2024) assessment of UK-specific priorities in recent negotiations.
Economic Adaptation Costs Having already adjusted to Brexit, businesses would face another round of disruption and adaptation costs if the UK rejoined. Confederation of British Industry (Business Investment Survey, 2024) documenting £20 billion spent on Brexit adaptation; British Chambers of Commerce (Economic Transition Analysis, 2023) estimating costs of readjustment to EU rules.

Fake Claims and Common Misconceptions

This section addresses widely circulated falsehoods or misleading arguments related to the UK, Northern Ireland, Brexit, and EU rejoining debates, providing evidence-based refutations from authoritative sources.

Claim: "The UK sends £350 million per week to the EU"

Reality: This claim, famously displayed on the Vote Leave campaign bus, was misleading. The UK's gross contribution was approximately £350 million per week, but this ignored the UK's rebate and EU spending in the UK.

Evidence: The UK Statistics Authority (Sir David Norgrove, Chair, 2017) officially stated this figure was a "gross misuse of statistics" as it did not account for the rebate or money returned to the UK through various EU programs.

Claim: "EU membership prevents the UK from making its own trade deals"

Reality: While EU membership does mean trade policy is conducted at the EU level, the collective bargaining power of the EU has historically secured more favorable terms than the UK could achieve alone.

Evidence: Post-Brexit trade deals have largely replicated existing EU arrangements. The Department for International Trade (UK Government, Official Report, 2024) acknowledged that new trade deals have provided only marginal benefits compared to previous EU arrangements.

Claim: "Northern Ireland has been fully separated from the UK due to the Protocol"

Reality: While the Northern Ireland Protocol created some trade barriers between Great Britain and Northern Ireland, Northern Ireland remains constitutionally part of the UK with representation in the UK Parliament.

Evidence: The Windsor Framework (UK Government and European Commission, Official Agreement, 2023) significantly reduced checks and paperwork while maintaining Northern Ireland's place in the UK's constitutional order.

Claim: "The EU is planning to create a European army that would replace national forces"

Reality: The EU has developed defense cooperation initiatives, but these complement rather than replace national armed forces. All major defense decisions require unanimous agreement from member states.

Evidence: The European Defence Agency (Official Policy Document, 2025) explicitly states that defense cooperation respects the sovereignty of member states and their specific character in security and defense policy.

Claim: "Rejoining the EU would force the UK to adopt the euro"

Reality: While new EU members are technically required to commit to eventually joining the euro, there is no fixed timeline, and several long-standing EU members still use their national currencies.

Evidence: Countries like Sweden (EU member since 1995) have indefinitely postponed euro adoption without penalties. The European Central Bank (Policy Statement, 2024) confirmed that euro adoption requires meeting specific convergence criteria and national readiness.
